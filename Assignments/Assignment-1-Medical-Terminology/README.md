# Assignment 1: Medical Terminology

## Overview

This assignment identifies and explains key medical and healthcare terms found in selected journal articles and reports focused on pregnancy, preterm birth, neonatal intensive care, and data-driven approaches to perinatal health.

## Approach

Fourteen terms were selected from research summaries and peer-reviewed studies. Each entry provides a concise definition, shows how the term is used in context within the source material, and explains why the concept matters for communication, patient safety, and clinical decision-making. The terminology selections emphasize pregnancy and neonatal care as areas of ongoing personal research and learning.

## Key Terms Covered

1. **Preterm birth** — delivery before 37 completed weeks of gestation
2. **Gestational age** — how far along a pregnancy is, measured in weeks
3. **Preeclampsia** — pregnancy-related disorder with new-onset high blood pressure after 20 weeks
4. **Placental dysfunction** — impaired placental development or function
5. **Clinical decision support tool (CDST)** — software providing patient-specific assessments
6. **Biomarker** — measurable biological feature indicating a physiological state
7. **Interleukin-33 (IL-33)** — inflammatory signaling molecule involved in immune regulation
8. **Digital biomarker** — physiologic or behavioral measure collected via digital devices
9. **Heart rate variability (HRV)** — variation in time interval between successive heartbeats
10. **Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD)** — chronic lung condition in very premature newborns
11. **Neonatal intensive care unit (NICU)** — specialized hospital unit for critically ill newborns
12. **Chest radiograph** — imaging test using X-rays to visualize lungs and thoracic structures
13. **Electronic health record (EHR)** — longitudinal digital record of patient clinical information
14. **SNOMED CT** — comprehensive clinical terminology standardizing diagnoses and procedures

## Key Takeaway

Consistent, standardized terminology is essential for reliable clinical communication and for building AI systems that depend on accurately labeled healthcare data. In neonatal care, where conditions change rapidly, precise language directly supports patient safety and research reproducibility.
