# Assignment 6: Exploring Non-Traditional Health Data

## Overview

This assignment explores social determinants of health (SDOH) as a form of non-traditional health data that describes the social and environmental conditions influencing people's health and life expectancy.

## Approach

The World Health Organization defines SDOH as the conditions in which people are born, grow, work, live, and age. The assignment examines three common examples — housing stability, access to nutritious food (food insecurity), and education and economic opportunity — and how they relate to health outcomes. It also reviews trends in SDOH screening at U.S. physician practices, which rose from 15% in 2017 to 27% in 2022.

## Key Findings

- SDOH data can improve health outcomes when health systems use it to identify needs early and connect people to support
- Both individual-level screening and neighborhood-level indicators (such as area deprivation measures) provide useful but different information
- Unfavorable social conditions are linked to higher risks of disease and death, including accelerated biological aging

## Ethical Concerns

- SDOH questions can feel personal and collecting them poses privacy and security risks
- Inaccurate SDOH data can increase stigma or distrust, particularly among groups that have experienced discrimination
- False positives can lead to unnecessary interventions; false negatives can cause real needs to be missed
- "Screening without help" — asking about needs without offering support can frustrate patients and widen inequities

## Key Takeaway

SDOH data are important because they explain health risks that clinical tests alone cannot capture. When used carefully, with patient consent, robust data protections, and clear pathways to assistance, SDOH information can help prevent illness, reduce disparities, and make health care more responsive to people's lived realities.
